A growth-stage company under LP scrutiny. The question on the table: do we need to replace executives? The real answer was buried somewhere else entirely.

The brief · The process · The finding · The fix
The mandate
A classic brief — with a charged question underneath
Significant growth financing had just closed. LP visibility was high, pressure on the portfolio company was rising, and the lead investor needed an answer: is this management team capable of what comes next? The original ask was a standard management assessment — personality diagnostics, 360-degree reviews, deep interviews with every executive and every board investor.
C- and Director-level scope
The process
Weeks of data. An unexpected picture.
Psychometric assessments across the full executive team. 360-degree reviews to surface blind spots. Deep interviews with all investors on the board to align expectations. The data came back comprehensive — and it pointed somewhere no one had expected. Not at the team.
Psychometrics · 360° · Board interviews
The finding
The team was fine. The system around them was broken.
No competence gaps. No personality mismatches. The executives could do the job. The real bottleneck was a pressure cascade running invisibly through the hierarchy: a senior fund partner generating pressure — transferring to the lead investor — who passed it with best intentions directly to the CEO. That single dynamic had overloaded the investor–CEO relationship and was blocking everything downstream.
Critical finding
The intervention
One protected 1:1. A complete recalibration.
In a confidential session with the lead investor, the dynamics were surfaced and named. The awareness that followed wasn't comfortable — but it was the turning point. The investor recalibrated: learned to manage expectations upward more sovereignly, and removed the pressure from the CEO relationship entirely. That single shift unlocked what the whole assessment had been searching for.

New CEO. New CFO straight from the fund's investment team. A buy & build mandate. And a so-called leadership team of 15 that had never made a hard decision together.

Diagnosis · Restructure · Sparring · The hidden dynamic
First offsite · August
The first offsite told me everything I needed to know.
Fifteen people, freshly assembled post-transaction. Technically the "senior leadership team." In practice: strangers who happened to share a company, with no shared language for conflict and no track record of hard decisions together. My immediate diagnosis to the board and CEO — a steep transformation cannot be steered by a committee of 15. You need a small, trusted, decisive C-level. Everything else is noise.
Diagnosis delivered to board
The restructure
From 15 to 5. Radical focus.
Starting the following year, I worked intensively with the CEO to restructure the leadership team and align it around a single direction. 15 became 5–6: a real executive team with clear decision rights and a mandate to execute. The committee was gone. The C-level began.
Bi-weekly CEO sparring · Quarterly offsites
Four levers · Ongoing
Strategy, structure, network — and the question of leverage.
We worked four things relentlessly. First: what M&A targets fit the product strategy exactly, and what "fantasy" does the leadership team need to build today to make the next buyer pay a step-change multiple? Second: the shift to matrix organisation to integrate acquired assets — including hard calls on tech stack consolidations. Third: my network, deployed where external expertise was missing — two headhunters for the CPO search, change management firms for the matrix rollout. Fourth and most important: training the CEO on leverage. Which three decisions actually move the company? Everything else is distraction.
M&A · Matrix org · CPO hire · Performance culture
The hidden dynamic
The CFO came from the fund. The CEO had no one to think out loud with.
In any PE-backed structure, the CEO is systemically isolated. Here it was amplified: the CFO had come directly from the investor's own team — meaning the investor was effectively sitting at the C-level table. The CEO couldn't raise doubts about executives with the board without signalling weakness. Couldn't evaluate with HR. Had to play every CFO conversation carefully. I was the external safe room — where we could be completely honest about executive performance, necessary replacements, and the reorganisations nobody else could know about yet.

I was behind the person in the room.

A founder-led tech company. A €400M merger to PE. Two years, three non-delegable phases — and a kind of advisory that most firms don't offer, because most firms don't understand what founders actually need.

Two years · Three phases · Key decisions mapped
Phase I · Year 1
The CEO who had to go — but couldn't
The founder had installed an external CEO to lead the company toward exit and begin stepping back. The constellation wasn't working. The decision to remove him was long overdue — but emotionally charged, legally complex, and organisationally treacherous. My role wasn't to manage the HR process. It was to be the space where the founder could finally confront the actual question: what do I actually want here, and what am I prepared to do to get it?
Critical decision point
Phase II · Year 1–2
The founder who had to stop running the company
An internal promotion to new CEO — high symbolic value inside the organisation. The founder's instinct was to re-intervene. That instinct, unchecked, would destabilise any leadership team regardless of how well it's otherwise configured. This phase was about building a genuinely new identity: non-operating owner. Stabilising the founder in that role was inseparable from building a leadership team capable of carrying the transaction.
Hardest phase psychologically
Phase III · Year 2
Shadow advisory in the exit process
As the sale process accelerated, the mandate shifted again. I wasn't part of the negotiations. I was behind the person leading them. Negotiation strategy and personal load-bearing happened in the same sessions. How the combined management team from both companies was structured and positioned wasn't an HR question — it was a valuation question with direct impact on the deal. That work happened here, not in the boardroom.
~400M€ transaction closes
What made this possible
The decisions that can't be delegated need somewhere to land.
In a transaction of this complexity, certain decisions cannot go to the lawyer, the banker, or the CFO — because each optimises for a partial view. The lawyer manages liability. The banker manages deal speed. The CFO manages the numbers. None of them holds the company, the negotiation, and the person carrying both simultaneously. That's what this mandate was. It drifted by design between the organisation and the founder. That's not a weakness in the brief. That is the brief.
